1. Session 5 summary: Continuing Day 5. The morning began with maps unrolled across the bastion’s war table, inked rivers and mountains stretched across the parchment like veins. Dusk Watch, it turned out, lay in the South—not where Samsons had expected—and two roads led there. One wound eastward and then turned south, the other struck straight through the marshlands. Both would take the same time, though the marsh road was cheaper, the ferry road safer. Before leaving, Samsons made one last stop at the academy. He asked the Dean, Miss Alura, for information about the rat they had found. “I’m heading to… Dusty Waters,” he explained. The Dean frowned. “There is no place by that name.” She put his name on the list for mandatory geography lessons. Still, she gave him what he wanted: a copy of the rune carved into the rat’s burned hide. At the keep, the Captain gave the company their final briefing. Scouts sent south had gone missing—Edrin, Lisa, Tobias, Salen. All had vanished, one after another, in the past twelve days. Each carried the raven-marked brooch of the Scout Watch. Along with them had disappeared vital shipments: moonshade moss, wagons of timber, crates of silver ore, barrels of lantern oil, and a cart of salted grain and fish. The party left the gates with four horses—two mounted, two pulling a carriage stocked with army rations for seven days. Kyron and Samsons took the driver’s seat, Samsons sitting back with the smug ease of a wizard certain the world was his to command. Merios and Ivor rode their own mounts. Scrap clanked inside the carriage. Queek clung to one of the draft horses, more passenger than rider. The first day passed without trouble. A bridge guard waved them through, the river glittering beneath. In the woods, wolves howled.
    
2. Soon they spotted another campfire on the road ahead—two traders from Briarford, roasting supper beside their wagon. Merios struck up a conversation, buying a clutch of apples for two silver. The traders shared a rumor: strange things in Dusk Watch. Their wagon had cast a shadow when no sun shone, then the shadow vanished. Uneasy but polite, the party moved on. That night they camped by a watchtower ringed with tents. Merios stretched her wings and flew to its peak, while the others pitched tents and shared rations. Watches were set, dreams were kind, and the night passed quietly. Day 6. The next day brought them to Briarford, a riverside village of three hundred souls. The ferry across cost four gold for them all, but it led only to the opposite bank, not southward to Dusk Watch. Worse, no one knew the cost of the ferries further downriver. Samsons argued for the marsh road—it was cheaper, simpler—but the rest chose the ferry route. The wizard fumed but followed, muttering about wasted coin. They stayed the night at the inn, drinking beer (all but Scrap), then set out at dawn. The marshlands were wet, grey, and smelled of rot. Along the way they discovered an abandoned cabin, water lapping against its frame. Inside, Queek found a barrel packed with fish and grain—rotting together into a foul slurry. One of the missing shipments, no doubt. They left it, marking the place in memory. That night they camped on a hill. Rain fell. Watches were taken. Samsons heard only the patter of rain. Scrap, with mechanical precision, picked up a gurgling sound far out to sea, but could not rouse the others. Queek promptly fell asleep on his watch. Ivor found him snoring and carried on. The gurgle came again—then a shadow darted across the water and fled. Scrap awoke the camp. Samsons and Kyron went out into the storm. Magic shimmered faintly in the waves, but when Samsons fixed his senses on it, the presence slipped away, retreating into the deep.
    
3. They returned, unsettled, and went back to sleep. Then came the dream. Each one of them, alone in a rowboat. The sea black as ink. Shadows leaping, crashing onto the boat, pulling them down, drowning. They awoke gasping, hearts pounding. Samsons and Kyron reeled from the nightmare, left exhausted. Day 7. By noon they reached a riverside dock—but no ferryman waited. They found him in a tavern instead, a burly captain nursing ale with his mate. “Dusk Watch?” the captain scoffed. “Ghost town. Dangerous waters. Sixty gold a head, if you’ve the coin to risk your souls.” Samsons nearly leapt out of his seat. “Sixty gold? For each? We could have crossed for four! Four!” Merios tried to charm the man, failed, and nearly started a brawl before storming out in fury. Samsons recovered the negotiations, coaxing the captain down to forty-five gold each. The man agreed to ferry them south and wait two days at sea for their signal. And so they returned to the docks, the price stinging, the waters restless. Ahead lay Dusk Watch, where the missing shipments, the vanished scouts, and the rune-burned rat’s secret waited.

    Day 8. The voyage toward Dusk Watch should have been uneventful—should have—but fate rarely respects such expectations. Before the boat even reached shore, the waters themselves offered grim portents: barrels of lost shipments bobbing like bloated corpses, accompanied by something far worse—an undead scout, half-rotted, half-drowned, and wholly hostile. Its jaw unhinged, spilling inky black ichor that writhed like something alive. Even after the creature was struck down, leeches slithered out from its remnants, clinging to planks and boots with nauseating eagerness. After dispatching these heralds of misfortune, the party entered Dusk Watch just as the lone tower bell tolled—a hollow, lonely sound in a town already too quiet. Their first stop: the chapel. Inside, they found a single Priest looped in a mantra of terror, whispering, “Light save us…” like a man trying to convince himself the Light still cared. Samsons, not one to waste opportunity, grandly impersonated a divine messenger—and the poor Priest believed him utterly. With a trembling finger, the Priest permitted their search. When Samsons revealed the faint glimmer of illusion magic, the altar itself betrayed a secret: a scroll of necrotic power, hidden beneath the guise of holy sanctity. From the chapel to the storehouse, the dread deepened. More barrels of missing cargo were found, though still incomplete—and among them the corpse of Lisa, the third missing scout… this one decidedly, irrevocably dead. No undeath. No second chances. Then, at the fountain square, the impossible occurred. The company saw themselves—an eerie procession of shadowy doppelgängers, silhouettes warped by magic, gliding silently along the cobblestones before fading into thin, hungry air. Samsons strained his senses: undeniably magical, undeniably wrong, and maddeningly indefinable. Seeking shelter—and answers—the group entered the town’s inn. What awaited them was not safety, but a place frozen in time:
