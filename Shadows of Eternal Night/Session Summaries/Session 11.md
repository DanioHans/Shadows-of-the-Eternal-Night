    Day 9. The square filled with sound once the silence broke. People cried openly. Some collapsed where they stood. Others clutched one another as if afraid the world might vanish again if they let go. Dusk Watch had returned to itself, but it was a wounded thing now. Hunger showed in hollow faces. Exhaustion bent backs. Whatever had held them in its grip had not released them gently. A small child approached us, her eyes red and searching. “What happened?” she asked. “I can’t find my father.” Merios knelt before her. “Your father poured our drinks perfectly,” she said softly. “He followed his routine… and the cellar flooded. I’m so sorry.” Samsons added his own cruel clarity, casting a spell to be certain. “He is dead. He is still in the cellar.” The girl did not cry. She only nodded once and ran toward the inn. We followed soon after. Merios and Queek meant to retrieve the body, but the child had already done so, dragging her father into the street with desperate strength. Rations were passed out from the inn’s doorway. We helped where we could, but time pressed upon us. The ferryman would not wait another night. Before leaving, we went to the mayor. His house stood closed. It had been open the night before. Merios knocked. An elderly man answered. He blinked at us as though seeing strangers in a dream. She handed him another ration, and he invited us inside, apologizing for the mess. He did not remember making it. Samsons told him of the food we had left at the docks for the townsfolk. Then he asked what had happened before the curse fell. The mayor spoke of a researcher from the Arcanum. A student, not so young and not so young-minded either. He had been studying anomalies for extra credit. Samsons frowned. “There is no such course.” The mayor did not know the student’s name. When asked about the chapel bell and its missing clapper, he shook his head. He had not even known it was broken. “What do you think happened?” the mayor asked.
    
7. Samsons hesitated. “Old magic. Powerful magic.” The man introduced himself at last. Elrich Thorn. With the sun sinking, we turned to the grim work of collecting the dead. The scout in the fountain was fused to the rubble. Only a bucket remained, its pages ruined by water. We could still read fragments. “Initial assessment. Anomaly detected. Not normal.” Merios and Kyron retrieved another body near the watchtower. An elderly man sat nearby, confused and staring at nothing. Merios patted his back with her tail and told him all was well. They carried the corpse away wrapped in a bedroll. At the storage house, Scrap and Queek lifted collapsed crates. Beneath them lay the third scout, crushed flat. Samsons shoveled what remained into a container with disturbing enthusiasm. “For research,” he said. Before leaving, Samsons found what he had come for. Moonshade moss. He held it up like a trophy. At the pier, the boat still waited. On board, Samsons read the scout’s remaining notes aloud while Ivor and Scrap played chess. “Residents show repetitive actions. Shared dreams. Cognitive influence rather than spell.” Queek stopped listening halfway through. We sailed to South Port and did not linger. The bodies were tied to horses and carriage. We traveled through the night. Scrap saw nothing on first watch. Queek startled at a deer. Samsons encountered a would-be robber and chased him off with magic and a floating slap. Then Samsons grew bored. “You let him drown,” he whispered into Merios’ mind. She screamed awake, convinced the nightmare had returned. By morning, Briarford welcomed us again. Merios tripped Samsons for complaining about the expensive route. Scrap paid for the inn. The road carried us onward through watchtowers and camps. Along the way, Samsons began writing a novel. The Lusty Infernal Maid. No one asked for details.
    
8. At last, the gates of Caer Hollow rose before us. The guards searched our packs. From Samsons’ bag, a shard of black and purple crystal emerged. Samsons stared at it. “I don’t remember putting that there.” “What is it?” the guard demanded. “Arcanum research material,” Samsons said confidently. “Mine.” The guard hesitated, then waved us through. At the tower, the captain awaited us. She listened to our report without interruption. When we finished, she nodded once. “You succeeded.” Gold was placed in our hands. Two hundred per person.
