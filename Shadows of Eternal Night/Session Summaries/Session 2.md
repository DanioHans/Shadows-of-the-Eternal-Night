1. Session 2 summary: Day 2. Morning broke in the Crown’s Bastion, but light did not reach the cells deep below. In their silent, alarm-fitted rooms, the party stirred—alone, with no way to whisper plans or trade jests. Sergeant Malma, gruff and purposeful, roused them from their sparse beds and led them deeper into the keep’s stone heart. Queek, ever the opportunist, turned to Samson with an unexpected question: “Will you be our lawyer?” The scholar, a legal trainee of the Royal Arcanum, reluctantly accepted the role, adjusting his collar as the group entered a sparse, candlelit room of stone and scrutiny. Across the table sat Captain Malma, and beside her a new face—Reno, a scribe-sharp auditor with piercing eyes and a cold demeanor. One seat remained open: for the High Arcanist, Samson’s superior, and not someone known for warmth. The questioning began, with Queek as the curiosity of the hour. A Skaven—unknown, unnatural, and utterly foreign. Queek told his tale: of Skavenblight, Clan Mors, and the doomed battle through the Skittergate. A gate torn open by the machinations of Chaos had flung Queek into this strange new world. Seeing the fight at the market, he’d simply joined in, hoping to prove himself among these strange creatures. While his blade had found no purchase, he had remained standing—a feat in itself. Reno shifted focus: Where had the basilisk come from? Who was the stall owner? He hinted that this wasn’t the first time dangerous creatures had slipped into the city. The slums offered plenty of tunnels and shadows to hide in. Maybe the stall owner escaped there. Scrap and Ivor stepped in—yes, they had seen the creature break loose, a lock falling from the cage after some sort of explosion. Yes, the stall owner had been present. Reno raised an eyebrow. “Strange, you all said before you didn’t know where the creature came from.”
    
2. He produced a sketch of a gaunt, slouching man. Scrap confirmed it was him. Samson rose to his new role, objecting to Reno’s tone and speculative accusations. “The guards were seen walking away,” he added, “perhaps more at fault than we.” Reno countered coolly: “Even guards can be afraid.” Queek, ever practical, added: “If fear is a reason, why not run, like the rest?” At last, the High Arcanist entered, clad in regal robes adorned with the city’s crests. He praised Samson—if barely—for returning the scroll. He doubted Samson had the capacity to cause such chaos, which was less compliment, more insult. Eventually, terms were offered: Find Jesper Rant, the smuggler and owner of the basilisk stall. Bring him back alive. In return, a clean record, and 50 gold pieces per person. Guards would be informed of the party’s mission—no questions asked. The slums, however, were dangerous. Reno briefed them: Two gangs: the petty Mall Rats and the more menacing Greyspite Cult. Unlicensed magic users roam freely. (Samson wondered aloud if they could be killed without consequence. The answer was... noncommittal.) The Severed Mug, a bar accepting outsiders—though known for its bar fights. Housing was scarce, privacy even more so. Jesper Rant was described: gloves always on, hunched from a foldable cage strapped to his back, thin, human, brown-haired. Merios, disinterested in gold, requested a cloak adorned with city sigils as his reward—something earned, not bought. Reno promised it would be his. With the agreement made, the party split briefly. Merios stayed behind to tell Silas and Damen, his adoptive parents, farewell at the Brass Ladle Inn. The others prepared for the task ahead.
    
3. Weyland Blades, Arcane Ledge, Salter & Sons, and Three Threads and Needles—these names passed among the party like whispered directions. They would need to blend in before stepping into the underbelly of Caer Hollow. Samson broke from the group and entered the Arcane Ledge, a narrow, crowded shop bursting with order and chaos all at once. Behind the counter stood a wizard who looked like he hadn’t slept in a week, yet still ran his store like a library. When Samson revealed the basilisk eye in its jar, the wizard recoiled slightly, muttering, and threw a cloth over it. He recognized the eye’s worth, especially for alchemical uses, and offered a deal: one lesser Stoneskin potion in exchange, plus 25 gold. Samson agreed—he signed a liability waiver with a flourish and left the shop with a potion sloshing in his pack. Elsewhere, the rest of the group stepped into Three Threads and Needles, a modest tailor’s shop with bolts of fabric stacked high and dust catching the light through shuttered windows. The shopkeeper took one look at the strange assortment before him—winged tiefling, masked rogue, silent warforged, and a skaven in a cloak—and narrowed his eyes. He was ready to turn them away until Merios stepped forward, calm and composed, and explained that they were investigating the market incident on behalf of the city.
    
4. That, and Samson’s timely arrival, gave the group some much-needed legitimacy. Samson claimed they were on a research mission for his thesis on magical devices. The lie held. Scrap received a worn hood riddled with tiny holes and the faint smell of mildew. Merios was measured and fitted for a tailored cloak that flowed over his wings, hiding his radiant armor and spear. The fabric shimmered ever so slightly, prompting the shopkeeper to examine it—armor that glowed but held no temperature. Fascinating, but no discount. Merios paid two silver without complaint. Their disguises in place, the group made their way southwest, into the narrowing streets of Caer Hollow. The air grew heavier with soot. Buildings leaned inward, old timber and rusted nails giving the streets an uneven spine. Children darted barefoot across broken cobbles. Windows were shuttered. Faces turned away. A small group of broad-shouldered men watched them pass. “You don’t belong here,” one muttered, but they pointed them onward. Two minutes’ walk to the Severed Mug. The party walked faster and made it there in one. Outside the tavern, a thick-armed bouncer blocked the door. Weapons, he grunted. All of them. Grudgingly, the party handed over their gear. Merios tried to conceal a blade, but the bouncer’s hands were thorough. Samson took the key to the weapons locker, tucking it into his robes. Inside, the Severed Mug hit the senses like a blow. The smell of boiled cabbage curled up the nose. Voices shouted across the room in overlapping dialects. A haze of sweat and spilled beer hung under the low ceiling.
    
5. The barkeep stood behind the bar, silent, expressionless. When Samson asked for drinks, the man raised three fingers. Merios passed over a silver—no change came back. Samson swept the room with his eyes, catching every shadow and shifting figure, but no one resembled the slouching man they were hunting. No sign of Jesper Rant. Food was ordered next. The barkeep raised five fingers. Samson paid a gold and asked for a bottle of strong spirits. Bowls of soup appeared—cabbage, potatoes, some kind of meat. Fixen the fox received a portion from Samson’s own bowl. While the others ate, Ivor disappeared upstairs. He moved like a shadow. One door, then another. Behind one, snoring. A quick twist of his tools, a soft click (23), and the door creaked open. The room inside was worse than expected: a lumpy bed, a broken chair, the air stale and heavy. He left it untouched and vanished back downstairs (18). At the bar, Merios began his line of inquiry. He handed the barkeep two gold pieces. Then another. Then one more. Still, the man said nothing. Samson, watching, cast Message—allowing Merios’ voice to pass through him. That broke the silence. The barkeep spoke in riddles, voice as dry as dust: “The lizard handler drinks cloudy fire when the moon leans west. Coin runs backward through his hand, as his debt sleeps where leather rots.” Ivor leaned in, murmuring his interpretation. He drinks late. He prefers strong liquor. He’s careless with coin, swimming in debt. But “where leather rots”… that was still a puzzle.
