# Court Reference – DM Procedure Guide

> This note is for running the courtroom, not just understanding it. It covers procedure, flow, objections, and what to do when players try something unexpected. Keep this open during Session 16.

---

## How a Session of Court Actually Flows

Think of the court as having a **strict turn order**, like combat but slower. Reno controls the floor. No one speaks without being recognised. The sequence is:

### 1. Opening — Reno establishes the session

He confirms who is present, states the charges, and asks the accused to enter their answer. This is administrative. Players cannot meaningfully intervene here. Let it run.

### 2. Accusation Phase — Oswin presents, witnesses are called

Oswin gives his formal statement. Then he calls witnesses one at a time. Each witness:

- Gives their account uninterrupted
- Can then be questioned by Oswin (rarely — he already knows what they'll say)
- Can then be questioned by the defence (the party)

The party's job during accusation phase is to **listen and find the gaps**. They should not be trying to argue their case yet — that comes later. If they try to interrupt a witness mid-testimony, Reno will stop them. _"You will have the opportunity to question the witness when they have finished."_

### 3. Defence Phase — party presents their case

After all accusation witnesses have been heard, Reno invites the defence. The party can:

- Call their own witnesses (including themselves and Queek)
- Present evidence
- Make direct arguments to Reno

This is where the bulk of the session's roleplay lives.

### 4. Reno's Questions — he fills gaps himself

After both sides have spoken, Reno may ask his own questions of anyone in the room. He is not on either side. He is trying to understand what happened. His questions are often the sharpest in the room.

### 5. Closing — Reno deliberates and rules

He may deliberate briefly (in silence, or by stepping out for a few minutes in-fiction). Then he returns with his verdict. No more arguments at this point — the floor is closed.

## Controlling the Floor — Reno's Tools

Reno is the DM's mouthpiece for keeping order. He has several moves available:

**"You will be recognised in turn."** — Someone tries to speak out of turn. Reno acknowledges them but does not give them the floor yet. They have to wait. Use this to build tension rather than just shutting players down.

**"That is a statement. Do you have a question?"** — A player tries to argue during cross-examination instead of asking questions. Reno redirects. Cross-examination is questions only — the party makes their arguments during the defence phase.

**"Noted."** — Reno writes something down. He does not respond. This is useful when a player makes a point that is valid but premature, or when you want to acknowledge something without resolving it yet.

**"The court will take a brief recess."** — Use this when things get heated, when the party needs a moment to strategise, or when you need time to think. A recess is diegetically normal. It also gives players a chance to talk amongst themselves, which is valuable.

**"Remove yourself from the witness stand."** — Someone on the dais is being evasive, hostile, or refusing to engage. Reno ends their testimony. This can happen to party members too if they push too hard.

**"One more outburst and you will be removed from this chamber."** — Final warning before a player character is physically escorted out. Use sparingly but mean it.

## Objections — How They Work Here

Players will absolutely try to object. This is good — it means they're engaged. The key is that objections are not automatic interruptions. They are **requests for Reno's attention**.

### How to handle an objection:

1. Player says _"Objection"_ or equivalent — _"Wait, that's not right"_, _"Your Honour, I need to speak"_
2. Reno decides whether to hear it. He almost always will, briefly.
3. The player states their grounds in one or two sentences.
4. Reno rules: **sustained** (objection accepted, testimony or question stopped), **overruled** (objection rejected, continue), or **noted** (acknowledged, no immediate effect).

### Common objection types and how Reno rules:

|Objection|What the player is saying|Reno's likely ruling|
|---|---|---|
|**"That's not what happened"**|Witness is factually wrong|_"Do you have evidence to the contrary? Save it for cross-examination."_ Overruled for now, but opens a cross-examination opportunity|
|**"That's an assumption, not a fact"**|Witness is stating an interpretation as fact|Sustained if the player is right — Reno will ask the witness to distinguish what they saw from what they concluded|
|**"The witness can't know that"**|Witness is speaking outside their direct experience|Sustained — Reno asks the witness to limit themselves to what they personally observed|
|**"That's irrelevant"**|Something is being raised that has nothing to do with the charge|Reno uses his judgement — if it genuinely isn't relevant, sustained; if it is relevant but the party doesn't like it, overruled|
|**"He's lying"**|Direct accusation of false testimony|Reno will note the Zone of Truth is in effect and ask the player to demonstrate the falsehood rather than assert it — _"Make your case during cross-examination"_|

### The important rule: objections slow things down, they don't win cases.

A sustained objection removes a piece of testimony from the record or stops a bad question. It does not change the overall picture on its own. Players need to follow up an objection with good cross-examination to actually use the opening they created.

## Cross-Examination — The Party's Main Tool

This is where the session's best moments will happen. Cross-examination is the party's opportunity to question accusation witnesses directly. Rules:

- **Questions only** — no speeches, no arguments. If a player starts arguing instead of asking, Reno redirects them.
- **One questioner at a time** — the party should decide amongst themselves who leads. Multiple people shouting questions is chaos; Reno will shut it down.
- **The witness must answer** — but on the Zone of Truth dais, they can be evasive. Clever follow-up questions are how you corner someone.
- **You can only ask about what they testified to** — players cannot introduce entirely new topics during cross. Save new information for the defence phase.

### The thing to reward:

Players who listen carefully during testimony and catch a specific inconsistency or assumption, then ask a precise question that forces the witness to walk something back. This is the Ace Attorney move. When it happens, let it land — have the witness visibly recalibrate, have Reno write something, let the room shift.

### The thing to discourage:

Players who try to use cross-examination to give speeches or argue their case. Redirect them firmly but without punishment — _"That is your argument, not a question. You will have the opportunity to make it during the defence phase."_

## Evidence — Can the Party Present It?

Yes, during the defence phase. Physical evidence can be introduced and examined. Things the party might try:

**Their Crownwatch service record** — Valid character evidence. Reno will accept it as relevant to the party's standing but will note it speaks to them, not to Queek directly.

**The fact that Queek stopped when Merios spoke** — This is argument, not physical evidence, but it can be corroborated by witness testimony. Pell Gorvan already confirms this independently; the party should point Reno to that testimony explicitly.

**Queek's nature as a displaced creature** — Argument supported by testimony. If Reno is asked whether a creature with no knowledge of this world's laws can be said to have formed criminal intent, he will consider it seriously. This is the strongest legal argument available.

**Anything from their missions** — Probably not relevant to this case. Reno will say so if they try.

## Things Players Will Definitely Try — and How to Handle Them

### "I want to Persuade the judge"

Persuasion checks are valid but they are not a substitute for argument. Tell the player: make your case first, then roll. A good argument with a bad roll lands better than a bad argument with a good roll. A great argument with a great roll is what shifts the outcome significantly.

### "I cast [spell] to help"

Any spell cast in the chamber is a separate offence and Reno will stop proceedings immediately. If it is a subtle spell (Sorcerer Subtle Spell metamagic) and they don't announce it, call for a Deception check against Reno's Perception (+4) and the cleric's Arcana (+varies). If caught, serious consequences. If not caught, let it stand but remember it — consequences can come later.

### "I want to Insight check Reno / Bryndis / the guards"

Entirely valid. Give them what they earn:

- Reno: he is genuinely undecided early on. He becomes more readable as the session progresses. High Insight (15+) can tell a player roughly where his disposition sits.
- Bryndis: she is angry and she is also telling the truth. Insight will confirm both. There is no hidden agenda to find.
- Corwin: nervous, earnest, slightly uncertain about the assumption he made. Insight 13+ picks up on the uncertainty around his "couldn't tell if she was breathing" claim.
- Pell: confident, slightly resentful of the magical chaos. No hidden agenda. What you see is what you get.

### "I want to call [unexpected witness]"

During the defence phase, the party can request to call witnesses Reno hasn't heard from. He will allow it if the witness is present or can be summoned quickly. Examples:

- The barkeep of the Brass Ladle — can testify that Queek had not been aggressive prior to the incident
- Merios as a witness rather than just a character advocate — valid and useful
- Someone from their Crownwatch history — Reno may allow it but will note it is peripheral

### "I want to argue there's no legal precedent for a Skaven"

This is a genuinely good argument and Reno will engage with it seriously. He will acknowledge that no precedent exists. He will then note that the absence of precedent does not mean the absence of a standard — the court applies the principle of intent regardless of the accused's species. The party's job is to argue that intent specifically cannot be established given Queek's context, not that the law doesn't apply.

### "Queek refuses to go on the stand" / "We won't let Queek testify"

Reno cannot compel testimony. However, if Queek does not speak, his testimony note — which is remarkable and genuinely moving — never enters the record. The court forms its picture from the guard accounts and Bryndis alone. This significantly worsens the outcome. Reno will note, for the record, that the accused declined to speak.

### "I want to attack / flee / cause a scene"

See the escalation section in the session document. Short version: the building is full of armed people and the party has no weapons. This ends badly.

## Pacing Notes

The trial should take most of the session. If it's running short, these are your extension valves:

- **Reno calls a recess** — natural pause, players strategise, you reset the room's energy
- **Reno asks his own questions** — he is sharp; his questions can open new angles the party didn't think of, prompting more discussion
- **Bryndis responds to Queek's testimony** — the single-line rebuttal (_"He says he thought he was in a war. I was not in a war. I was having supper."_) should land in silence; let that silence breathe
- **Samsons on the stand** — this scene has its own weight and should not be rushed; if it's going well, let it run
- **Reno's deliberation** — he can step out briefly before returning with the verdict; use this to build anticipation

If it's running long and you need to compress: Oswin's statement and Pell's testimony are the most cuttable. Corwin and Bryndis are essential. Queek's testimony is essential. Samsons is essential.

## Legal Terms — Quick Reference

**Hearsay** — A statement made outside the court, repeated inside it as evidence. Example: Oswin was not present at the inn — everything he says is hearsay, because he is reporting what the guards told him, not what he saw. Halna similarly. The party can object to hearsay, and Reno will acknowledge it, but hearsay is not automatically excluded — in this court it is treated as lower-weight evidence rather than inadmissible. The practical effect: a successful hearsay objection prompts Reno to note that the statement is unverified and should be corroborated by direct witness. It undermines Oswin's formal statement without erasing it.

**Direct testimony** — The opposite of hearsay. A witness speaking to what they personally saw, heard, or experienced. Corwin and Pell give direct testimony. So does Bryndis. So does Queek. This is the highest-weight evidence in the room.

**Leading the witness** — Asking a question that contains the answer you want, steering the witness toward a specific response. Example: _"You saw Queek try to kill her, didn't you?"_ rather than _"What did you see?"_ Reno will intervene if Oswin does this to his own witnesses, and will intervene if the party does it during cross-examination. Players should ask open questions and let the witness hang themselves.

**Speculation** — A witness stating what they believe, assumed, or interpreted rather than what they directly observed. Corwin's claim that he couldn't tell if Bryndis was breathing is speculation formed at distance — the party can object on these grounds and Reno will ask Corwin to separate observation from conclusion.

**Relevance** — Whether a piece of evidence or line of questioning actually bears on the charge. Reno will cut lines of questioning he considers irrelevant. The party should be ready to explain _why_ something matters if challenged.

**Remanded into custody** — Placed in a cell before a final verdict. Queek's current state.

**Custodial sentence pending further review** — Queek stays in a cell; the case is revisited in 30 days. No guaranteed outcome. The party can petition and visit during that time.

**Commuted sentence** — A punishment formally reduced or replaced. Requires authority to intervene (Felwin, later).

**Mitigating circumstances** — Facts that don't excuse the crime but reduce its severity. Queek's origin, instinct-driven state, lack of context, the fact that he stopped — all mitigating.

**Aggravating circumstances** — Facts that make the crime worse. Continuing after Bryndis was down. Resisting restraint. The magical chaos from a party member.

**Testimony** — Formal spoken account under the court's authority. Under Zone of Truth, treated as materially reliable.

**Character witness** — Speaks to the accused's general conduct and capacity for reform, not to the specific events.

**No precedent** — No previous case exists to guide the ruling. Cuts both ways: no standard sentence, but also Reno has broader discretion.

**Sustained** — Objection accepted. The testimony or question in question is stopped or struck.

**Overruled** — Objection rejected. Proceedings continue.

**Noted** — Acknowledged without immediate effect. Reno has heard it and it may factor into his thinking.
